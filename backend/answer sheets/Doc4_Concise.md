1. Force equals mass times acceleration. More mass requires more force to move. This principle governs all classical moving objects. It is derived from $F = dp/dt$. Since mass is completely constant, it becomes $F = m(dv/dt)$, which is simply $F = ma$. Example applications include calculating the required force for kicking a static soccer ball, launching heavy deep-space rockets, or pushing a broken car.

Demand quantifies a consumer population's capability and willingness to purchase a good at various prices, while supply represents the volume of goods producers are willing to manufacture and sell at those same price points. The Law of Demand states that as the price of an item increases, the quantity demanded by consumers universally decreases. Notable exceptions include Giffen goods, which are essential survival staples that paradoxically see higher demand as prices rise because consumers cannot afford alternatives, and Veblen goods, which are luxury items where high prices falsely signal superior status.
