1. Newton’s Second Law of Motion states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting upon it and inversely proportional to its mass. This principle forms the classical bridge between kinematics (how things move) and dynamics (why they move). The rate of change of momentum is $F = \frac{dp}{dt}$. Assuming constant mass throughout the process, this expands to $F = m \frac{dv}{dt}$. Since the derivative of velocity with respect to time is acceleration, this gracefully simplifies to the well-known $F = ma$. A practical example is pushing a heavy delivery truck compared to pushing a small bicycle. Pushing the truck requires a significantly larger net force to achieve the exact same acceleration due to its vastly greater mass.

2. A limit $L$ exists for a continuous function $f(x)$ as $x \to c$ if and only if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \epsilon$. This strictly evaluates proximity constraints. Consider the algebraic evaluation of $\lim_{x \to 2} (\frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2})$. By factoring, the discontinuity resolves to $4$. Derivatives logically and absolutely depend on this foundational construct via the difference quotient algorithm: $f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$.
